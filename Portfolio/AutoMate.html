AutoMate
Date: September - December 2017
Work Environment: Team of Five
Role: UX Designer

Summary

The purpose of this semester-long project was to design an interface for the inside of an autonomous car. It is apparent that the transportation industry is moving towards autonomous technology, but the front-facing interface poses novel issues, one of which being convincing people to trust this technology to drive for them. With AutoMate, we created an interface that features augmented reality and obstacle detection technology in an attempt to foster trust between passenger and car.

Problem Definition

People are wary of riding in autonomous vehicles because it requires them to let the car make the decisions. In particular, people do not fully know what the car sees or does not see, so people are worried that the car will make a fatal mistake. My team and I feel that autonomous technology offers the ability to substantially improve safety on the road, so addressing the issue of trust between people and autonomous vehicles could lead to wider adoption of autonomous vehicles and ultimately improve road safety.

Competitive Analysis

To start our project, we researched other autonomous technology (some related to the automotive industry and some not). Our goal was to see what other solutions chose to display, what functionality was offered, and if people trusted the technology enough to use it.

Ideation

From there, everyone in the team individually created 40 sketches of possible solutions to our problem statement. Though we wanted to design an interface, we wanted to investigate other solutions to see if there were aspects we wanted to incorporate in our solution.

Storyboards

We then used our personas to create storyboards. These demonstrated how our personas would interact with the proposed solution.

Paper Prototype

Then we moved on to low-fidelity prototyping to actualize some of the ideas we had been developing.



Afterwards, we realized our paper prototype focused too much on the infotainment and navigation features, which weren't experiences specific to autonomous vehicles. We moved on to digital prototyping, but with a focus on the obstacle detection display on the windshield and center console.

Digital Prototyping

My team struggled to find a tool that would facilitate windshield and console screen prototyping. We started with proto.io, but switched to using Adobe Illustrator/Invision later in the process. During this process, we created the Environmental Awareness Display (EAD) on the center console to give detailed feedback about what the car is sensing. We also added feedback about the signs the car is registering and how the vehicle will react to upcoming stoplights.

Final Design

Below is a video showcasing the different screens and scenarios our final design covered. The S1, S2, and S3 buttons trigger various scenarios, showing what the UI would display in various situations. The arrows in the upper right switch between external environments such as highway, urban, nighttime, and others. Check it out!


Final Thoughts

We created this design with the goal of inspiring trust in people who are apprehensive about riding in autonomous vehicles. While I am very proud of the design we ended up with, I wish we had been able to explore and prototype voice interactions more. Along with the detailed feedback on on the windshield and console, voice interactions could make the interface feel much more natural and allow passengers to regain the sense of control they are wary of giving up in autonomous vehicles. Nevertheless, our design offers a possible solution to some of the novel challenges autonomous vehicle interfaces must address.
