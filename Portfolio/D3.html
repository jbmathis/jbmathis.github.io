Data Driven Detroit Toolbox
Date: January - April 2018
Work Environment: Team of Four
Role: UX Researcher and Designer

Summary

This was my capstone project for my bachelor’s degree in UX design and took place over the course of my final semester. Since this was a group project, I collaborated with three other students from my class to complete assignments. Our project involved researching and redesigning the website for Data Driven Detroit (D3), a nonprofit organization aimed at providing high-quality data to drive informed decision-making in Detroit. Throughout the semester, my team had weekly meetings with D3 employees to discuss our findings, requirements, and progress. At the end of the project, we presented our final design to D3 and the rest of our class.

View our Final Report

Background Information

Our specific task was to redesign the Toolbox page of the D3 site, where users could browse and interact with the various datasets D3 offers. D3 wanted to ensure the information architecture and design was simple so users ranging in levels of data experience could easily interact with their resources. The D3 team was already developing a prototype for a new website, so they asked my group to analyze the prototype along with the current site to provide insight on how to improve the user experience.

Target Audience

Before we started our UX research, we worked with D3 to identify three target user groups and a guiding question on how to successfully cater to each:

Urban Planners


Does D3's Toolbox present resources in a manner that an urban planner could quickly and easily understand a desired urban area by identifying and exploring relevant resources?

Data Analysts


Does D3's Toolbox present resources in an understandable, yet informative manner so that experienced data analysts could quickly identify resources of interest?

Data Novices


Does D3's Toolbox present resources in an intuitive manner so that data novices can understand the resources D3 offers and how they could be useful?

Competitive Analysis

Our competitive analysis aimed to discover how other sites organized their content by comparing attributes such as items included in the header, the use of hero space, and structural organization of assets. We evaluated four sites in total. Two sites were directly similar to D3 (Western Pennsylvania Regional Data Center and Shift Research Lab) and two were indirectly related, but used notable methods of organizing content (Netflix and Kaggle). This activity helped us to understand different information architecture methods used inside and outside the industry.

Heuristic Analysis

Next, we conducted a heuristic analysis of D3’s current site and prototype to evaluate the usability of both portals. We followed the usability heuristics from Nielsen Norman Group’s 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design. After gathering and categorizing the issues, we rated the severity of each issue on a 1 - 5 scale agreed on ahead of time. Usability Tests

To validate the issues we identified, we ran five usability tests. We recruited users based on our three target user groups: one user was a professional urban planner, two were University of Michigan students with data and analytics experience, and two were students with no prior data experience. Since our goals and research questions were different for each user group, we drafted three different test scripts. In each, we asked users to complete tasks with D3’s current and prototype site, randomizing which site users tested first. Brainstorming Solutions

Next, we moved into the design phase of our project. We began by having brainstorming sessions using sticky notes and then sketching ideas that were the most compelling. Below are a few of our initial sketches for how to organize resources on D3’s Toolbox page. We were experimenting with either showing resources in a vertical list or as boxes shown horizontally. We also thought it would be helpful to institute some sort of rating system, so highly-rated resources would be pushed to the top.



We showed these sketches to D3 and our classmates to gather initial feedback. Some of the most notable feedback included that our layouts didn’t have to be mutually exclusive. For instance, we could display categories as a horizontal list and display the datasets inside them as a vertical list. It was also pointed out that a rating system where users can down-vote or dislike could make some resources unappealing. Instead, we were encouraged to consider a badge system to simply highlight exemplary resources.

Wireframing

With this initial feedback in mind, we moved to creating low-fidelity mockups. We conducted informal user testing to evaluate components in the context of the larger design. Feedback from these tests informed some of the changes we made between the paper and digital wireframe, like adding icons for different categories.

Validation Testing

We then tested our digital prototype against D3’s current site to validate that our design solved the issues we had identified. The user tasks included prompts such as filtering to view specific resources and locating datasets within a category. We used the metrics of average task completion time and average (user-indicated) task difficulty as the basis for our testing. As you can see below, our prototype out-performed D3’s site on all counts.

Final Design

Below is a screenshot of our final prototype. At the end of our project, we shared with D3 our final prototype along with a list of recommendations on how to improve the usability of their site. These recommendations included advice based on our research that was targeted towards the Toolbox page specifically, but also general UX best practices on how to design for usability.


Final Thoughts

This was an extremely demanding project because it required going through research, design, and validation phases all within one semester. While this meant we couldn’t devote as much time to certain tasks as we might have liked, it also meant we were involved in every phase. In the professional world, it can be rare for the same UX professionals to be involved in a project from start to finish. Sometimes a UX analyst is brought in to conduct research and hand off requirements to a graphic designer or a UI designer is given requirements to design an interface at the end of the project. Being responsible for the research, testing, and design not only taught me about how to execute each of these phases effectively, but also made me incredibly invested in and proud of the final product.
